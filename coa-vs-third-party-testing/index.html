COA vs third-party testing

A COA and a third-party testing claim are related, but they are not the same thing. One is the document, while the other describes how the testing is framed and who is said to have performed it.

Understanding the difference helps visitors read supplier pages more precisely.

COA

The document itself

A certificate of analysis is the report a visitor can open, read, and compare.

Its usefulness depends on readability, identifiers, dates, and method detail.

  • Visible report
  • Batch match
  • Date
  • Method and result
Third-party testing

The testing relationship claim

Third-party testing language describes who is said to have performed the work and how the supplier frames that support.

The phrase is more convincing when it leads directly to a readable COA or related report.

  • Named lab
  • Public-facing support
  • Batch context
  • Clear path to the file
How to use both

The best supplier pages make the relationship obvious

Strong pages do not force visitors to guess whether a third-party testing claim is supported by a real certificate. The document is linked, the batch is visible, and the language stays consistent.

That makes the comparison between suppliers much easier to trust.

Question

Is a COA the same as third-party testing?

No. The COA is the document, while third-party testing describes the testing claim and its source.

Question

Can a supplier mention third-party testing without showing a COA?

Yes, and that is one reason the claim may remain hard to verify.

Question

Why compare the two concepts on one page?

Because visitors often see them together and need to know how they differ.

Question

Which matters more, the phrase or the file?

The file usually matters more because it is the public evidence a visitor can inspect.

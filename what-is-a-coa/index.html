What is a Certificate of Analysis (COA)?

A Certificate of Analysis, commonly called a COA, is one of the most important documents researchers use when evaluating a peptide supplier.

A COA is intended to provide batch-specific information about a product, including identity, purity, testing methods, lot numbers, and testing dates. It does not guarantee quality on its own, but it gives researchers a clearer record of what testing was performed and what information is available for a specific batch.

Overview

Why COAs matter

Peptide suppliers often make claims about purity, testing, quality control, or manufacturing standards. A COA is one of the clearest ways those claims can be supported with documentation.

Without a COA, researchers may have little visibility into which batch was tested, when testing occurred, what methods were used, what results were reported, or whether documentation exists at all.

Many experienced researchers look for COAs before evaluating pricing, promotions, or marketing claims. A supplier that publishes clear, batch-specific documentation usually gives visitors more to work with than a supplier that only makes broad quality claims.

Usually included

What information commonly appears on a COA

  • Product name
  • Batch or lot number
  • Testing date
  • Laboratory information
  • Testing method
  • Reported results

Formats vary between laboratories and suppliers, but these elements usually determine whether a COA is easy to interpret and compare.

Key field

Product name

Identity

The COA should clearly identify the compound being tested, and the name on the report should match the name shown on the supplier's website.

Key field

Batch or lot number

Traceability

A batch number links the testing document to a specific production run. When the same batch appears on the product page, label, and COA, the document is much easier to evaluate.

Key field

Test date

Timing

A visible testing date adds context. It does not automatically make a report stronger, but it helps show whether the document appears current and relevant to the product batch.

Key field

Laboratory and method

Support

Named laboratories and clearly listed methods such as HPLC, LC-MS, or identity testing provide more context than anonymous reports with vague result language.

Purity

What reported purity does and does not mean

Purity is one of the most frequently discussed COA metrics. A result like 99.1% means that, according to the testing method used, approximately that share of the measured material matched the desired compound profile.

Purity is still only one data point. Researchers usually evaluate it alongside identity confirmation, batch documentation, testing date, laboratory information, and overall supplier transparency.

Reading context

How strong documentation looks in practice

Strong COA documentation creates a clear trail from the product page to the testing report. If the product page says Tirzepatide 10mg and lists batch TIRZ-250501, the COA should show the same compound and batch.

Weak documentation does the opposite. Generic PDFs, missing dates, no lab identity, or no batch number leave researchers with much less information to evaluate.

What a COA can tell you

Useful context for supplier comparisons

  • What batch was tested
  • When testing occurred
  • Which laboratory performed the testing
  • What methods were used
  • What results were reported
What a COA cannot tell you

Why a COA should not stand alone

  • Future batch consistency
  • Supplier reliability
  • Manufacturing practices
  • Customer service quality
  • Shipping quality
  • Overall company trustworthiness

This is why experienced researchers usually review COAs together with third-party testing, supplier transparency, policy pages, contact information, and research-use-only consistency.

Common red flags

What weakens a COA quickly

Missing batch numbers, anonymous laboratory reports, undated files, generic documents reused across multiple products, and purity claims without accessible documentation all reduce how useful a COA is for comparison work.

The issue is not always that the product is poor quality. The issue is that the visitor has much less information available to evaluate what is being claimed.

COA checklist

Questions worth asking when reviewing a report

  • Is the product clearly identified?
  • Is a batch number visible?
  • Does the batch number match the product page?
  • Is a testing date provided?
  • Is the laboratory identified?
  • Is the testing method listed?
  • Are results clearly reported?
  • Can the report be downloaded or viewed easily?
  • Does the documentation appear batch-specific?

Question

What does COA stand for?

COA stands for Certificate of Analysis.

Question

Does every peptide supplier provide COAs?

No. Some suppliers provide extensive documentation while others provide little or none.

Question

Is a COA the same as third-party testing?

Not necessarily. A COA is a document. Third-party testing refers to testing performed by an independent laboratory.

Question

Does a COA guarantee quality?

No. A COA provides useful information, but it should not be treated as a guarantee.

Question

Is purity the most important number on a COA?

Purity is important, but it is only one part of the overall documentation picture.

Question

Why are batch numbers important?

Batch numbers help connect testing results to a specific production run.

Question

What if a supplier does not provide a COA?

Researchers may have less information available to evaluate that supplier.

Question

Should researchers compare multiple COAs?

Yes. Comparing documentation between suppliers can provide additional context and help identify differences in transparency.

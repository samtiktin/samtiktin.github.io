How to Read a Peptide COA

A Certificate of Analysis (COA) is one of the most useful documents available when evaluating a peptide supplier. While many researchers know they should look for a COA, fewer understand how to interpret the information inside it.

Learning how to read a COA allows researchers to move beyond marketing claims and evaluate actual documentation. A supplier may advertise high purity, third-party testing, or strict quality standards, but the COA is often where those claims become easier to verify.

What is a COA? How to compare peptide COAs
Overview

Why learning to read COAs matters

Many peptide suppliers advertise high purity, third-party testing, laboratory verification, or quality assurance. A COA helps provide context behind those claims.

Researchers who understand how to read COAs can often identify missing information, weak documentation, batch mismatches, outdated reports, and generic testing files.

The goal is not to prove a supplier is good or bad from a single document. The goal is to understand how much useful information is available and how clearly that information connects back to the listing.

Step 1

Confirm the product name

Identity

The first thing to verify is that the product listed on the COA matches the product being sold. If the website and the report use different naming conventions, researchers should determine whether both refer to the same compound.

Step 2

Find the batch number

Traceability

The batch number is often the most important section of the document because it links the report to a specific production run. Researchers should check whether the same batch appears on the COA, the product page, labels, or related testing notes.

Step 3

Check the testing date

Timing

A visible date helps show when the analysis was performed and whether the report appears relevant to the current batch.

Step 4

Identify the laboratory

Source

A strong COA should identify who performed the testing. Named laboratories, report numbers, and contact details make a document easier to evaluate than an anonymous file.

Step 5

Understand the purity section

Purity is usually the most viewed part of a COA, but it should never be read by itself. Researchers should weigh purity alongside batch information, laboratory information, testing date, identity testing, and the method used.

A purity percentage without supporting context provides much less information than a complete report.

Step 6

Review the testing method

Many peptide COAs reference analytical methods such as HPLC, LC-MS, or mass spectrometry. Researchers do not need advanced chemistry knowledge to benefit from this information, but knowing which method was used helps explain how the result was obtained.

  • HPLC for purity evaluation
  • LC-MS for identity confirmation and molecular analysis
  • Mass spectrometry for molecular weight and compound identity
Step 7

Review the results table carefully

Many COAs summarize the core testing details in a table. The important thing is not only that results are present, but that the values are readable, the specifications are clear, and the information appears complete.

  • Appearance
  • Purity
  • Identity
  • Water content or related support values

Strong documentation

What a clear documentation trail looks like

Imagine a supplier lists Retatrutide 10mg on the product page with batch RETA-250501, and the COA shows Retatrutide, batch RETA-250501, a named laboratory, an HPLC method, a visible date, and a readable purity result.

That kind of alignment creates a consistent documentation trail researchers can actually follow.

Weak documentation

What leaves researchers with less to evaluate

A page that only says "99% pure" and "lab tested" while providing no batch number, no date, no laboratory name, and no downloadable report leaves researchers with much less usable context.

The problem is not only the claim itself. The problem is that the page offers very little information that can be checked.

Common COA red flags

Signs that documentation may be weak

  • Missing batch numbers
  • No testing date
  • No laboratory information
  • Generic COAs reused across multiple products
  • Purity claims without supporting documentation

None of these issues automatically settles the question of product quality, but each one reduces how much confidence a visitor can place in the public-facing documentation.

Quick checklist

Before trusting any COA, ask these questions

  • Is the peptide clearly identified?
  • Is a batch number listed?
  • Does the batch number match the product page?
  • Is a testing date provided?
  • Is a laboratory identified?
  • Is a testing method listed?
  • Are results clearly reported?
  • Is the document readable?
  • Does it appear batch-specific?

Question

What is the most important part of a COA?

The batch number is often one of the most important pieces of information because it connects the report to a specific production run.

Question

Is purity the only thing that matters?

No. Purity should be evaluated alongside laboratory information, testing dates, identity testing, and overall documentation quality.

Question

What if the supplier does not provide a COA?

Researchers have less information available to evaluate that supplier.

Question

Can two suppliers show the same purity percentage?

Yes. Purity alone does not determine overall documentation quality.

Question

Is a COA the same as third-party testing?

Not necessarily. A COA is a document. Third-party testing refers to who performed the analysis.
